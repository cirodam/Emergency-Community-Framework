# Epistemic Architecture

How the ECF encodes epistemic health into its governance structures.

The goal is not to tell people what to believe, but to create institutional conditions that make it harder to govern by fiction and easier to govern by evidence.

**Historical note — the Junto model:** Benjamin Franklin's Junto (1727) was a mutual improvement club for tradespeople that required members to bring questions, evidence, and reasoned positions to each meeting. Members were expected to articulate *why* they believed something, not just what they believed. Franklin himself adopted a deliberate rhetorical posture of uncertainty ("it seems to me...", "I may be wrong, but...") to model intellectual humility. The ECF's structured motion reasoning is a quiet institutional inheritance of that tradition — by requiring premises and expected outcomes, people practice empirical thinking without being lectured about it.

---

## 1. Structured Reasoning on Motions

Every motion states its premises explicitly before a vote opens. The reasoning field is not a description of what the motion does — it is a statement of what the proposer believes to be true that makes the motion necessary.

**Structure:**
- **Premises** — what factual claims does this motion rest on?
- **Proposed action** — what does the motion do?
- **Expected outcome** — what result does the proposer predict?

This separates the *claim* from the *vote*. The assembly can challenge the premises directly — not just approve or reject the conclusion — which produces higher-quality deliberation and a searchable record of what was argued.

**Why legislatures don't do this well:** Vague reasoning is strategically durable. Coalitions often form around bills for incompatible reasons. Stated premises create accountability. In a sortition-based community with short terms and no career incentive, these pressures don't apply.

---

## 2. Amendments Address Claims, Not Just Text

Amendments to a motion can target its *premises*, not only its operative text. A member can propose: "the premise that X is true is contested — amend to acknowledge uncertainty" or "the expected outcome is wrong — amend to state a more defensible prediction."

This makes deliberation substantive rather than purely rhetorical.

---

## 3. Dissent Recording

When a motion passes, dissenting assembly members may attach a formal minority statement. This is not a protest mechanism — it is an epistemic record. Dissents are searchable and linked to the motion permanently.

Communities that never record dissent never know what the majority overruled. When the outcome later proves the dissenters right, that information is lost. Recorded dissent closes that loop.

**Precedent:** Judicial minority opinions are the clearest example of this working well. Courts that require public reasoning — including dissent — produce more epistemically accountable decisions than bodies that do not.

---

## 4. Outcome Tracking / Retrospectives

A `MotionReview` links back to a resolved motion and records what actually happened relative to the stated expected outcome. Was the premise correct? Did the predicted outcome occur?

This is the feedback loop that separates communities that learn from communities that only argue. Without it, failed predictions have no institutional consequence. With it, members who consistently reason well become visible, and patterns of bad reasoning can be identified and addressed.

**Trigger:** Any member can propose a review of a resolved motion. Reviews go through the normal deliberation process.

---

## 5. Constitution Sections Track Their Origins

Each section of the constitution records which referendum established it and the reasoning from that referendum. Rules whose origins are forgotten become cargo cult rules — followed without understanding, immune to reconsideration.

Traceable origins allow the community to ask: "the situation that motivated this rule — does it still apply?"

---

## 6. Deliberation Period as Epistemic Space

The deliberation window before voting opens is explicitly framed as the period for presenting evidence and challenging premises — not lobbying. The assembly's role during deliberation is to examine the reasoning, not to advocate.

This is a cultural norm enforced architecturally: comments during deliberation are labeled as *evidence* or *challenges to premises*, not general discussion.

---

## 7. Source Attribution on Claims (future)

Long-term: claims made in motion premises can be linked to sources — community records, external references, prior motion outcomes. This creates a traceable chain from governance decisions back to the evidence that justified them.

This is not fact-checking by authority. It is transparency of reasoning that allows any member to follow the chain themselves.
